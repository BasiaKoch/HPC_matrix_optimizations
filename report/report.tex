\begin{abstract}
An in-place Cholesky factorization routine $C = LL^T$ is developed in C and
optimized in five stages for a single 76-core CSD3 icelake node.
Loop interchange and compiler flags (\texttt{-O3 -march=native -ffast-math})
yield a 13--16$\times$ single-thread improvement over the \texttt{-O0} baseline.
A flat OpenMP parallelization reaches at most 46.2~GFLOP/s at 48~threads for
$n=2000$, then \emph{degrades}, consistent with synchronization overhead
becoming dominant at high thread counts.
Restructuring into a right-looking panel-blocked algorithm (panel width
$\mathrm{NB}=96$) reduces the number of outer synchronization stages from
$n$ column steps to $\lceil n/\mathrm{NB}\rceil$ panels,
giving 59.5$\times$ speedup and 78.2\% efficiency at 76~threads for $n=8000$
(147.2~GFLOP/s).
Four additional optimizations---column packing, a private packed panel block,
4-wide SYRK unrolling, and round-robin scheduling---improve single-thread
performance by 2.52$\times$ relative to v4 at $n{=}8000$, and the final
version reaches 182.7~GFLOP/s at 76~threads.
\end{abstract}

% ======================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ======================================================================

Cholesky factorization $C = LL^T$ is a core operation in Bayesian inference,
Gaussian-process regression, and numerical optimization, where it is used to
invert and compute the determinant of symmetric positive-definite covariance
matrices.
For $n$ observations, the covariance matrix $C \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$
has entries $C_{ij} = \langle(x_i - \langle x_i\rangle)(x_j - \langle x_j\rangle)\rangle$,
and its log-determinant is given by
\begin{equation}
  \log|C| = 2\sum_{p=0}^{n-1}\log L_{pp}.
  \label{eq:logdet}
\end{equation}
The routine is $O(n^3/3)$ and must be both correct and fast for large $n$.
This report describes systematic optimization of a single-node OpenMP
implementation on CSD3~icelake, reaching \textbf{182.7~GFLOP/s} at 76~threads
for the large-size case $n=8000$ (absolute peak 238.4~GFLOP/s at $n=2000$).

% ======================================================================
\section{Algorithm and Interface}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Cholesky recurrence}

The factorization processes column $p = 0,\ldots,n-1$ in order.
At each step:
\begin{align}
  L_{pp} &= \sqrt{C_{pp} - \sum_{s=0}^{p-1} L_{ps}^2},
  \label{eq:diag}\\
  L_{ip} &= \frac{C_{ip} - \sum_{s=0}^{p-1} L_{is}L_{ps}}{L_{pp}},
  \quad i > p.
  \label{eq:col}
\end{align}
Steps~(\ref{eq:diag})--(\ref{eq:col}) are serial; the trailing Schur complement
update $C_{ij} \mathrel{-}= L_{ip}L_{jp}$ for $i,j>p$ is independent across
rows and is the primary target for parallelism.

\subsection{Function interface}

The function
\begin{verbatim}
double mphil_dis_cholesky(double *c, int n);
\end{verbatim}
takes a row-major $n\times n$ matrix, factorizes it in place, and returns the
wall-clock time in seconds.
On return, the lower triangle holds $L$ and the upper triangle holds $L^T$.
The routine rejects $n < 1$ or $n > 100000$ and returns $-1.0$.

\subsection{Correctness verification}

Correctness is assessed by comparing $\log|C|$ computed via~(\ref{eq:logdet})
against NumPy reference values for the corr-function test matrices at
$n \in \{5, 50, 200\}$, with agreement to within $10^{-6}$ relative tolerance.
The 2$\times$2 example from the coursework brief ($C = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}4&2\\2&26\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$)
is verified element-by-element.
All 38 test cases pass (\texttt{make test VERSION=v5\_openmp\_blocked NB=96}).

\section{Optimisation Strategy}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{v1 --- Baseline (tag \texttt{v0.1-baseline})}

The baseline follows the provided loop structure compiled at \texttt{-O0 -g}.
The innermost trailing update iterates over $j$ in the outer loop and $i$ in
the inner loop, so \texttt{c[i*n+j]} is accessed with stride $n$ in the inner
loop --- giving poor spatial locality for large~$n$ and likely increasing
cache-miss pressure.

\subsection{v2 --- Serial optimisation (tag \texttt{v0.2-serial-opt})}

Two changes are made, both single-threaded:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Loop interchange.}
    Swapping to outer~$i$, inner~$j$ makes \texttt{c[i*n+j]} stride-1 in~$j$,
    improving spatial locality and making vectorization of the inner loop more
    likely under the chosen compiler flags.
  \item \textbf{Loop-invariant hoisting.}
    \texttt{c\_ip = c[i*n+p]} is moved outside the $j$-loop; this value is
    invariant in $j$, so hoisting avoids a reload on each inner-loop iteration.
    The normalisation loops in v2 still use \texttt{/=~diag}; explicit reciprocal
    precomputation (\texttt{inv\_diag = 1.0/diag}, replacing $O(n)$ divisions with
    $O(n)$ multiplications per step) is introduced in v3.
\end{enumerate}
Compiled with \texttt{-O3 -march=native -ffast-math}, these changes give
13--16$\times$ speedup over v1 for $n = 500$--$2000$ (Figure~\ref{fig:serial}).

\subsection{v3\_openmp --- Flat OpenMP parallelism (tag \texttt{v0.3-openmp-v1})}

The trailing update rows are independent, so \texttt{\#pragma~omp~for~schedule(static)}
distributes them across threads.
A single parallel region is opened \emph{outside} the $p$-loop so the thread
team is created once and reused for all $n$ steps (\texttt{\#pragma~omp~parallel}).
\texttt{\#pragma~omp~single} serializes the diagonal and column normalization,
with an implicit barrier ensuring all threads see the updated values before the
parallel update begins.
\texttt{\#pragma~omp~simd} vectorizes the inner~$j$ loop.
v3 also introduces \texttt{inv\_diag = 1.0/diag}, replacing the
\texttt{/=~diag} divisions from v2 with multiplications in the normalisation loops.

At $n=2000$, v3 peaks at 48~threads (46.2~GFLOP/s) and then \emph{degrades}
to 33.7~GFLOP/s at 76~threads (Figure~\ref{fig:scaling}).
This behavior is consistent with synchronization overhead from the $O(n)=2000$
barrier points becoming significant: as thread count grows, the parallel work
per step ($\approx(n-p)^2/T$) may fall below the cost of the barrier
synchronisation, reducing utilization.
At $n=4000$ the problem is large enough that v3 saturates at 65.7~GFLOP/s from
64 to 76~threads (31.6$\times$ speedup, 41.6\% efficiency) without degrading,
but still far below linear.

\subsection{v4\_openmp\_blocked --- Panel-blocked algorithm}

Replacing the column-by-column outer loop with a panel-of-$\mathrm{NB}$-columns
loop reduces the number of synchronization stages from $O(n)$ column steps to
$O(n/\mathrm{NB})$ panels.
For $n=8000$, $\mathrm{NB}=96$ gives 84 panel iterations instead of 8000.
Each panel pass performs three phases:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Phase~1 (serial): diagonal block factorization.}
    \texttt{\#pragma~omp~single} serializes the $O(\mathrm{NB}^3)$ factorization of
    the $\mathrm{NB}\times\mathrm{NB}$ panel diagonal block.
    The implicit barrier at the end ensures Phase~2 sees finalized~$L_{11}$.
    The within-panel trailing update reads \texttt{c[j*n+p]} (lower triangle)
    rather than \texttt{c[p*n+j]} (upper triangle, stale for $k>0$).

  \item \textbf{Phase~2 (parallel): TRSM below the panel.}
    \texttt{\#pragma~omp~for~schedule(static)} distributes rows $i \geq k_\mathrm{end}$
    across threads.
    Each row computes $\mathrm{NB}$ forward-substitution steps using the
    finalized diagonal block; work per row is relatively uniform, making static
    scheduling a natural choice.

  \item \textbf{Phase~3 (parallel): SYRK trailing lower-triangle update.}
    \texttt{\#pragma~omp~for~schedule(guided)} distributes the triangular update
    $C_{ij} \mathrel{-}= \sum_p L_{ip}L_{jp}$ for $j \leq i$.
    Work per row increases linearly with~$i$; this guided policy does not always
    balance the increasing triangular load optimally (see v5 OPT-4).
    \texttt{\#pragma~omp~simd reduction(+:dot)} vectorizes the inner dot product.
\end{enumerate}

The panel width was tuned empirically for \texttt{v5\_openmp\_blocked} by sweeping
$\{64,96,128,192,256\}$ at $n=8000$ and thread counts $\{1,8,32,76\}$
(Figure~\ref{fig:blocksweep}). The optimum is thread-dependent:
NB$=96$ gives the highest mean throughput at 1~thread (6.24~GFLOP/s) and
76~threads (187.8~GFLOP/s), whereas NB$=256$ is best at 8~threads
(37.7~GFLOP/s) and 32~threads (97.9~GFLOP/s). This is consistent with a tradeoff
between barrier overhead and cache locality. Larger panels reduce the number of
panel iterations from 84 at NB$=96$ to 32 at NB$=256$, which helps at
intermediate thread counts. At full-node occupancy, however, the smaller panel
regains a slight advantage; NB$=256$ remains very close at 186.9~GFLOP/s, so the
full-thread optimum is broad. Because the main strong-scaling study uses a
single fixed kernel configuration, NB$=96$ is retained as the default setting
for the report.

\subsection{v5\_openmp\_blocked --- Microarchitectural optimisation}

Four targeted optimisations are added over v4; the algorithm and barrier count
are unchanged. Their combined effect is measured directly in the benchmarks
below; the individual OPT-1--OPT-4 mechanisms are motivated from the code
structure and access patterns, but they are not isolated by separate one-factor
ablation runs in this report.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{OPT-1: Column packing (Phase~1).}
    \texttt{c[j*n+p]} in the within-panel update has stride $n$~doubles
    (64~KB for $n=8000$), which may cause L2/L3 misses at large~$n$.
    Pre-copying into \texttt{double col\_p[NB]} converts those reads to stride-1.

  \item \textbf{OPT-2: Private per-thread packed panel block (Phase~2).}
    Phase~2 TRSM reads \texttt{c[p*n+s]} with stride $n$ between rows of the
    diagonal block; for NB=96 rows this spans $96\times64$~KB~$=$~6~MB, exceeding
    L2 (1.25~MB/core).
    Declaring \texttt{double l11[NB*NB]} inside \texttt{\#pragma~omp~parallel}
    (private to each thread) and packing the $\mathrm{NB}\times\mathrm{NB}$
    lower triangle after Phase~1 produces a 73-KB contiguous copy that fits
    within L2, intended to reduce long-stride accesses in the TRSM inner loop.

  \item \textbf{OPT-3: $j{\times}4$ SYRK unrolling (Phase~3).}
    Processing four output columns simultaneously with four independent
    accumulators \texttt{d0..d3} breaks the FMA dependency chain.
    The icelake core has two AVX-512 FMA units (8-wide, 2 FMAs/cycle); a single
    reduction accumulator creates a dependency chain that may underutilise them.
    With four independent accumulators, \texttt{\#pragma~omp~simd~reduction(+:d0,d1,d2,d3)}
    gives the compiler the opportunity to improve SIMD and instruction-level
    parallelism.

  \item \textbf{OPT-4: \texttt{schedule(static,1)} (Phase~3).}
    Round-robin row assignment gives each thread an interleaved mix of heavy
    (large~$i$, many $j$-values) and light rows, giving better load balance
    than a contiguous assignment for the linearly-increasing triangular workload.
    This scheduling change is introduced together with OPT-1--3, so its effect
    is not isolated separately; it is included because it is consistent with the
    triangular work profile of Phase~3.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{v3 saturation and degradation.}
At $n=2000$, v3 peaks at 46.2~GFLOP/s (48~threads) and degrades to
33.7~GFLOP/s at 76~threads.
Efficiency is only 14.2\% at 76~threads.
The factorization performs $n=2000$ column steps, each ending with thread
synchronization; the parallel work per step ($\approx(n-p)^2/T$) likely falls
below the barrier overhead
at high $T$, consistent with the observed efficiency of 14.2\% at 76~threads.
With the newly completed large-$n$ curves, the same implementation reaches only
26.3~GFLOP/s at $n=6000$ and 12.9~GFLOP/s at $n=8000$ at 76~threads,
substantially below both blocked versions; this is consistent with the flat
algorithm's high synchronization frequency and weaker cache locality.

\paragraph{v4 near-linear scaling.}
At $n=8000$, v4 achieves 59.5$\times$ speedup and 78.2\% efficiency at
76~threads (147.2~GFLOP/s), with near-linear scaling to 32~threads.
Each of the 84 parallel-work panels replaces approximately NB=96 individual
column steps from v3, substantially reducing synchronisation frequency.

\paragraph{v5 microarchitectural gain.}
At $n=8000$, v5 achieves 182.7~GFLOP/s at 76~threads (+24\% over v4).
Its single-thread performance is 2.52$\times$ higher than v4 (6.25 vs.\
2.47~GFLOP/s), consistent with improved data locality and SIMD efficiency.
The lower reported parallel efficiency of v5 (38\%) compared to v4 (78\%)
should not be read as worse parallelism: v5 is faster than v4 at every tested
thread count, but efficiency is normalized by single-thread performance, so a
faster single-thread baseline reduces the ratio.

\section{Discussion}
% ======================================================================

\paragraph{Amdahl analysis.}
v3's limited scaling at $n=4000$, 76~threads ($31.6\times$, 41.6\% efficiency)
likely reflects two effects: repeated serial sections in each iteration, and
the overhead of 4000 synchronization-heavy column steps.
Although the trailing update dominates the total flop count asymptotically,
the per-step serial work and synchronisation overhead become increasingly
visible at high thread counts.
v4 largely alleviates both effects by reducing the serial work to $O(\mathrm{NB}^3)$
per panel and replacing 4000 column steps with only 42 panel iterations at
$n=4000$.

\paragraph{Super-linear speedup.}
v3 at $n=4000$ shows super-linear speedup at 2--8~threads (8.30$\times$ at
8~threads).
The full matrix (128~MB) exceeds the combined node L3 ($\approx$114~MB); as thread count grows,
the per-thread working set decreases, which may improve cache reuse and reduce
memory traffic relative to the single-thread case.
This appears to be a cache-capacity effect rather than algorithmic
super-linearity; the margin above ideal speedup is small and may partly reflect
measurement variation.

\paragraph{v5 efficiency interpretation.}
The lower reported parallel efficiency of v5 (38\%) compared to v4 (78\%)
might suggest v5 parallelizes worse.
That interpretation would be misleading on its own: efficiency is normalized by
single-thread performance, so a faster single-thread baseline lowers the reported
ratio even when absolute parallel throughput improves.
In these results, v5 is faster than v4 at every tested thread count (0.94~s
vs.\ 1.16~s at 76T), so the lower efficiency does not indicate a worse
implementation.
However, it may also reflect earlier saturation of shared bottlenecks, so
absolute runtime should be the primary measure.

\paragraph{Memory-bound vs.\ compute-bound.}
For small $n$ (2000), the matrix ($n^2 \times 8 = 32$~MB) fits within a
single socket's L3 (57~MB) and the computation appears more compute-bound;
v5 achieves 238.4~GFLOP/s at 76T.
For large $n$ (8000, 512~MB), the working set far exceeds the combined node
L3 ($\approx$114~MB) and throughput falls to 182.7~GFLOP/s, consistent with
a shift toward memory-system bottlenecks.
The NB=96 packed panel block ($96 \times 96 \times 8 \approx 74$~KB) is
designed to keep Phase~2 data in L2, but the full matrix cannot fit in cache
and memory bandwidth likely becomes a ceiling at large $n$.

% ======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ======================================================================

Starting from a 0.26~GFLOP/s naive baseline, five development stages reach
183~GFLOP/s at 76~threads for $n=8000$ on a single CSD3 icelake node,
corresponding to an overall throughput increase of roughly 700$\times$
relative to the naive baseline.
Loop reorder and compiler flags contribute 13--16$\times$ on a single core.
Panel blocking greatly reduces the synchronisation overhead seen in v3,
achieving 59.5$\times$ speedup and 78.2\% parallel efficiency.
Additional locality, SIMD, and scheduling optimisations (v5) improve
single-thread throughput by 2.5$\times$ and parallel throughput by a further
+24\% at full thread count.
For large matrices, performance appears increasingly limited by memory-system
throughput rather than computation.
